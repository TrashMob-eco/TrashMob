## Body

Picking up litter by yourself is fine. Picking up litter with a crew is better. That's the idea behind Teams — one of TrashMob's core features for turning individual cleanup volunteers into organized, motivated groups.

Whether it's a group of neighbors, a corporate volunteer squad, a scout troop, or a college environmental club, Teams gives your group an identity, a shared track record, and a reason to keep showing up.

### Creating a team takes two minutes

Go to the Teams page, click Create, and fill in the basics: team name, description, and location. Upload a logo if you have one — it shows up in the team directory and on your team page. Set your team to public (discoverable by anyone) or private (invite-only). Choose whether new members can join freely or need approval from a team lead first.

That's it. Your team exists. Now fill it with people.

### Recruiting members: one at a time or all at once

There are three ways people join your team:

**Direct join.** If your team is public and doesn't require approval, anyone can find it in the team directory or on the map and join with one click.

**Join request.** If your team requires approval (or is private), users submit a join request. Team leads see pending requests and can approve or reject them.

**Bulk email invites.** This is the one that saves time when you're onboarding a whole group. Enter email addresses — one per line or comma-separated — and TrashMob sends branded invitations to each person. They get a link to join your team directly. You can see the history of every invitation batch you've sent.

If you're bringing in a corporate volunteer group, a church group, or a neighborhood association, bulk invites get everyone onboarded in one shot instead of texting each person individually.

### Team events: clean up together, track impact together

Teams can create cleanup events or claim existing events as team initiatives. When team members attend an event linked to the team, the impact data — bags collected, weight removed, hours worked — rolls up to the team's collective totals.

Your team page shows upcoming events so members know what's next, and a history of past events so everyone can see what the team has accomplished. Over time, this builds a track record that motivates the team and demonstrates impact to anyone considering joining.

### Leaderboards: friendly competition that actually works

Here's the feature that keeps teams coming back: leaderboards.

TrashMob ranks teams by four metrics — events participated in, bags collected, weight of litter removed, and volunteer hours logged. You can view rankings for the past week, month, year, or all time.

The leaderboard page shows the top 50 teams with their ranking position and score. Teams need at least three combined event attendances to qualify, so you can't game the rankings by creating an empty team. Rankings refresh daily.

There's something about seeing your team at #7 and knowing #6 is only two bags ahead that makes people organize one more cleanup. We've seen it happen repeatedly — teams that plateau in activity suddenly ramp up when a rival team passes them on the board.

Individual volunteers have their own leaderboard too, so team members can compete within the team while the team competes externally. It's layered motivation.

### Team photos: show your work

Every team gets a photo gallery. Upload pictures from your cleanups — before-and-after shots, group photos, particularly impressive hauls of trash. Add captions to tell the story behind each image.

Photos go through the same moderation pipeline as everything else on the platform to keep content appropriate, but the turnaround is quick. Your approved photos show up on your team page and give prospective members a visual preview of what your team does.

Team leads can also upload or update the team logo at any time, so your team's branding stays current whether you're using a casual group photo or a designed logo.

### Leadership without the overhead

Teams have two roles: member and lead.

**Members** participate in events, view team info, contribute to collective stats, and appear on leaderboards.

**Team leads** get the management tools: edit team settings, approve join requests, send bulk invites, manage photos, promote other members to co-leads, and link events to the team. You can have multiple leads, so the organizational burden doesn't fall on one person.

One restriction: team leads must be 18 or older. Minors can be team members and fully participate, but the lead role requires an adult — a sensible default for an organization responsible for coordinating in-person activities.

### Finding teams near you

The team directory offers two views: a searchable list and an interactive map.

The list view shows every public team with its name, location, visibility status, and whether it requires approval to join. The map view plots team locations as pins — hover over one to see team details, click to visit the team page.

If you're new to TrashMob and looking for a group, the map is the fastest way to find teams in your neighborhood. If you don't see one nearby, that's your cue to start one.

### Team adoptions: ongoing responsibility for an area

For communities running adopt-a-street, adopt-a-park, or similar programs, teams can apply to adopt specific areas for ongoing cleanup responsibility.

The workflow: browse available areas on the community map, submit an adoption application with notes about your team's plans, wait for the community admin to review and approve. Once approved, your team is responsible for maintaining that area at the required cleanup frequency.

The system tracks compliance automatically — event count, last cleanup date, whether you're on schedule. If your team falls behind, the community admin sees it in their dashboard and can follow up before the area becomes a problem.

This connects Teams directly to the adopt-a-location system we covered in the previous blog post. Teams are the organizational unit that makes long-term area stewardship work.

### Why teams matter for program managers

If you're running a community cleanup program, Teams are your force multiplier. Instead of managing hundreds of individual volunteers, you work with organized groups that have their own internal leadership.

Teams self-organize — they recruit their own members, schedule their own events, and hold each other accountable. Your job shifts from herding individuals to supporting team leads. That scales far better than direct volunteer management.

The leaderboard adds external motivation that you don't have to generate yourself. And the adoption system gives teams a concrete long-term commitment instead of just "show up when you feel like it."

### Start a team today

Whether you're three friends who clean up your local park on Saturdays or a 50-person corporate volunteer group running quarterly cleanup days, Teams gives you a home on TrashMob with tools to recruit, organize, track impact, and compete.
